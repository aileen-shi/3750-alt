Perspective

Introduction

"Life is about perspective and how you look at something... ultimately, you have to zoom out.

-Whitney Wolfe Herd"

Perspective is used to represent 3-dimensional objects in a 2-dimensional space. You can use it to create the illusion of depth for the viewer.

Elements of Perspective

Horizon Line

The horizon line is where you are eye level. It is the point where the sky meets the ground and you cannot see past it. A higher horizon line makes it feel like you are looking down. A lower horizon line will feel like you are looking up.

Vanishing Point

The vanishing point is where parallel lines converge to on the horizon line. Everything gets smaller as it recedes into the distance and they will appear to merge into a vanishing point.

Orthogonal Lines

Orthogonal lines lead to a vanishing point. They are imaginary lines used plane out edges not directly facing the viewer.

Ground Plane

The ground plane is the horizontal surface or ground under the horizon line. It can be a good reference for placing items down.

Converging Lines

Converging are the parallel lines that seem to merge at the vanishing point. They help create a sense of depth.

Linear Perspective

One-Point Perspective

One-point perspective is when you look at something straight on. There will only be one vanishing point. Anything facing directly towards will have the bottom and side edges be vertical and horizontal. Edges on the side will point towards the vanishing point.

Two-Point Perspective

Two-point perspective has 2 vanishing points on the horizon. You will be facing the corner of something rather than face on. Edges on the left will point towards the left vanishing point and vice versa for the right.
