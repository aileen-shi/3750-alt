Principles of Composition

Introduction

"A composition is always more than the sum of its parts. In other words, a really good piece of music is more than itself. It's sort of like a prism, which you can see from each facet a single totality."

-Yo-Yo Ma

The principles of composition are the guidelines for organizing and arranging elements of design within a piece.

Balance

Balance describes the visual weight inside a composition. It is used to create a sense of stability and harmony.

Types of Balance:

  • Symmetrical: Elements are mirrored around an axis
  • Asymmetrical: Elements are not mirrored and vary in sizes, shapes, and colors.
  • Radial: Elements are arranged around a central point radiating outwards.

Movement

Movement can be used to guide a viewer's eyes. Movement can be created using lines shapes and colors.

Rhythm

Rhythm uses elements repeatedly in a way that creates motion similar to music. Types of rhythm include regular rhythm which is consistent, alternating rhythm which alternates between multiple elements, and progressive rhythm which gradually changes elements as they repeat.

Emphasis

Emphasis helps to create the focal point of a work. The focal point is the area that grabs your attention first. You can emphasize your focal point through contrast, placement, or size and scale.

Contrast

Contrast uses differing elements to draw your attention. For example, you can use contrasting colors to make elements stand out against each other. You can also use different shapes or forms to create contrast. Textures and patterns like something shiny vs something dull will also create contrast.

Repetition

Repetition repeats elements to create a sense of unity. Repetition is like rhythm without the different patterns.

Proportion

Proportion is the size and scale of elements in relation to each other in a composition. Scale is the size in relation. Hierarchy is the order of importance of elements. Usually elements are seen as more important than smaller ones.
